2. The Scorecard Method
Setup: "Here's how we're going to figure this out. I've identified the 6 most important factors that determine how fast you actually get good at boxing. We're going to evaluate each learning method across these factors, then see which one comes out on top."
The 6 Categories Explained:
Feedback: How quickly and accurately you get corrected when you're doing something wrong. This is the most important factor - you can't improve what you can't see.
Time Invested: How much of your time gets wasted vs. how much actually contributes to your improvement. The more efficient your time, the more you can invest and exponentially improve faster.
Price: Not just the upfront cost, but what are you actually paying for each bit of progress? Plus the hidden cost of bad habits - paying money to learn things you'll have to pay even more to unlearn later.
Information Quality: Are you learning vague techniques they haven't questioned, or systematic first-principles? Is this opinion-based ("this worked for me") or grounded in biomechanics that actually works? Has this coach thought about what they're teaching from first principles, or are they just repeating what someone repeated to them?
Community Quality: Do you have access to other people who accelerate your learning? But here's the key - if the information quality is wrong, the community becomes an epistemic tribe that reinforces bad knowledge until it becomes gospel. You end up with entire gyms teaching the same flawed approach because they're all reinforcing each other's mistakes.
Sparring Access: Can you test what you're learning under pressure? But more importantly - and this is crucial - are you actually prepared when you do? Your body's natural tendencies to box are completely wrong, and if you keep sparring with those natural tendencies, you won't be able to get out of those bad habits. This is extremely common in gyms that don't have the other categories sorted - they throw you in unprepared and you actually get worse.
